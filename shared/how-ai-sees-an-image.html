How AI Sees an Image

Just like it chops text into tokens, AI chops a picture into patches, turns each patch into a list of numbers (the colours), and passes them through a neural network that decides what the picture shows. Pick an image and watch it happen in 3D.

Four things to take away

A picture is just numbers

Every pixel is three numbers — how much red, green and blue. A small photo is millions of numbers. AI only ever does maths on those numbers.

It works in patches, not pixels

Modern image AI cuts the picture into a grid of patches (like image "tokens"), then looks at the patches together — the same idea as tokens in text.

It finds patterns, then a label

Layers build up from edges and colours to shapes and objects, ending in a best-guess label with a confidence score — never certainty.

Confidence isn't correctness

"Stop sign, 91%" is a probability, not proof. Odd lighting, angles or unusual images can fool it — always sanity-check what it claims to see.

Mini glossary

Pixel
The smallest dot in an image, stored as three numbers: red, green and blue (RGB).
Patch
A small square block of the image the model treats as one unit — the image equivalent of a token.
Feature
A pattern the network learns to spot — an edge, a colour, a texture, and later whole shapes.
Attention
How each patch weighs up the other patches, so the model links related regions of the picture.
Classification
Choosing the most likely label for the whole image from the patterns found.
Confidence
The probability the model assigns to its answer — high confidence is not the same as being right.
